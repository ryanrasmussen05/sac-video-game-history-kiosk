1947 - 1954
  • 1947
    Thomas T. Goldsmith, Jr. and Estle Ray Mann invent the cathode-ray tube amusement device, the first interactive electronic game. Players control a beam with knobs and aim it at plastic targets. Though patented, the device never advances beyond a prototype.
  • 1950
    Josef Kates unveils Bertie the Brain, a machine that pits players against an artificial intelligence in a cutthroat game of tic-tac-toe. After its presentation in the Canadian National Exhibition, Bertie is dismantled and forgotten about.
  • 1951
    For the 1951 Festival of Britain, Ferranti premiers Nimrod, a computer that plays a virtual game of Nim. Like its predecessor Bertie, Nimrod was only meant for exhibition and dismantled afterwards.
  • 1952
    Cambridge student A. S. Douglas programs a game called OXO for a thesis. Though it is a simple game of tic-tac-toe like Bertie, it is one of the first games to utilize an electronic screen display, rather than light bulbs. After Douglas demonstrates it, he wipes the memory from the hardware.

1955 - 1964
  • 1958
    Brookhaven National Laboratory displays Tennis For Two, a virtual tennis game designed by William Higinbotham. It is the first video game made purely for entertainment.
  • 1962
    Steve Russell develops Spacewar! for the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. Eventually, it is installed on other computers of the same type at other academic institutions, making it the first video game available at multiple locations. 1965 - 1971
  • 1968
    Doug Dyment develops The Sumer Game, wherein players control grain management as the ancient Babylonian king Hammurabi. This is one of the first strategy video games.
  • 1971
    Nutting Associates releases Computer Space, the first commercial video arcade game. Simultaneously, Bill Pitts and Hugh Tuck demonstrate their Galaxy Game at Stanford University, using surplus B-52 controls as handles and jukebox coin slots to collect money. From now on, video game enthusiasts would have to pay to play.
